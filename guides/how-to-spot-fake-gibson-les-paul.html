Buyer's Guide

How to Spot a Fake Gibson Les Paul: A Collector's Guide

Let's be direct: counterfeit Gibson Les Pauls have gotten genuinely good. Five years ago, a fake was obvious from across the room. Today, there are counterfeits that can fool people who've been collecting for a decade. I've seen them at guitar shows. I've seen them listed on Reverb with real-looking COAs. I've had friends buy them.

When you're spending $4,000 to $20,000 on a guitar, you need to know what to look for. Not because you should be paranoid — but because the people making fakes are counting on you not knowing this stuff.

This guide covers 10 things to check, ordered from what you can spot in a listing photo to what requires the guitar in your hands. Some of these will seem basic. Some of them are things even experienced collectors miss. All of them matter.

The 10 Things to Check

Ordered from what you can check in a photo to what requires the guitar in front of you. Start at the top and work your way down. If something fails at number 2, you don't need to get to number 10.

01
Headstock Shape
Easy

This is the first thing to check and the most commonly botched detail on a counterfeit. A real Gibson Les Paul has an "open book" headstock shape with very specific proportions. The curves are symmetrical, the ears are rounded with consistent radius, and the overall profile is narrower than most fakes get it.

What fakes get wrong: The ears are too wide, too flat, or slightly uneven. The overall shape looks "bloated" compared to a real Gibson. The tip of the headstock (where it comes to a point between the ears) is often too sharp or too blunt. Some counterfeits get close, but if you overlay a real headstock template, the differences show up immediately.

What to do: Find a high-resolution photo of the model year you're looking at from Gibson's archives or a trusted reference like Gruhn's Guide. Compare the headstock shape side by side. Pay special attention to the width at the widest point, the radius of the ear curves, and the depth of the center dip.

Pull up the listing photo and a reference photo side by side on your phone. Pinch-zoom to the same headstock size. If the proportions feel off, they probably are.
02
Logo and Inlay Quality
Easy

The Gibson logo on the headstock is one of the most telling details on any Les Paul. On an authentic Gibson, the logo is either an inlaid mother-of-pearl or pearloid piece (on higher-end models) or a silkscreened decal. The font is specific and consistent within each era. The most distinctive detail: the dot on the "i" in Gibson is always a diamond shape, not a circle. On a fake, it's often round or just slightly wrong.

What fakes get wrong: The font weight is off — sometimes too thick, sometimes too thin. The letter spacing varies from the original. The "b" and "o" don't have the exact right proportions. The pearl inlay looks plasticky or doesn't have natural depth variation. On headstock inlays like the crown (Les Paul Custom), the fake version often has visible seams, uneven edges, or material that catches light differently than real pearl.

What to do: Ask for a close-up photo of the logo in natural light. If the seller can't provide one or it's oddly cropped, that's telling. Compare the font against known-authentic examples from the same era.

The "Les Paul Model" silkscreen below the logo is equally important. On authentic guitars, it's clean, evenly applied, and in the correct gold or white tone for the era. On fakes, it often flakes, bleeds, or sits at a slightly wrong angle.
03
Truss Rod Cover
Easy

This sounds minor. It isn't. The truss rod cover on a real Gibson Les Paul is a 2-ply piece (black over white on Standards, 3-ply black-white-black on some models) held by 2 screws on Standards or 3 screws on Customs. The shape is specific: a slightly tapered bell shape on Customs, a simpler shape on Standards.

What fakes get wrong: The ply count is wrong. A 3-ply cover on a Standard is a red flag. The screw count doesn't match the model. The shape is subtly off — too wide, too narrow, wrong taper. On a real Gibson, the edge of the truss rod cover sits flush with the headstock surface. On many counterfeits, it sits slightly proud or has visible gaps.

If the truss rod cover says "Custom" but other details don't match a Custom, or if it says "Standard" but has Custom appointments, start asking harder questions.

04
Serial Number Placement and Stamping Quality
Medium

Gibson serial numbers follow specific formats depending on the era and factory. Modern Gibsons (1977-present) use an 8-digit format where the 1st and 5th digits indicate the year of production, and digits 2-4 indicate the day of the year. The number is stamped (impressed) into the back of the headstock on most models.

What fakes get wrong: The stamping depth is inconsistent — sometimes too deep, sometimes barely visible. The font doesn't match the era. The placement is slightly too high or too low on the headstock. Some fakes use real serial numbers copied from authenticated guitars, so a "valid" serial number is not proof of authenticity. The physical quality of the stamping matters more than the number itself.

What to do: Check the serial number against Gibson's date decoding system. Verify that the number format matches the claimed era. Then look at the stamping itself — on a real Gibson, the impressions are even, cleanly struck, and consistent in depth. On a fake, individual digits may be different depths, slightly crooked, or the wrong typeface for the era.

Gibson also stamps "Made in USA" below the serial number on guitars made from the mid-1970s onward. The font, spacing, and depth of this stamp should match the serial number. If one looks different from the other, something's wrong.
05
Binding Quality and Nibs
Medium

This is a big one. On an authentic Gibson Les Paul, the binding on the fretboard extends slightly over the fret ends, creating small "nibs" where each fret meets the binding. These nibs are a hallmark of Gibson's production process. Most counterfeits skip this step entirely — it's time-consuming and hard to replicate.

What fakes get wrong: No binding nibs at all — the frets run all the way to the edge of the binding. The binding itself has visible seam lines, inconsistent width, or a plastic look that doesn't match the slightly yellowed, organic appearance of real Gibson binding. On the body, the binding may pull away from the wood at the cutaway or have uneven thickness around curves.

What to do: Ask for a side-on photo of the fretboard edge. You should be able to see the binding nibs clearly. If the seller's photos conveniently never show the fret ends, ask specifically. On body binding, look at the corners of the cutaway — that's where cheap binding fails first.

Some legitimate Gibson models (like certain Gibson USA standards from specific years) were produced without nibs. Know which years and models had them and which didn't before you use this as a dealbreaker.
06
Pickup Rings and Hardware Quality
Medium

Gibson uses specific pickup rings, bridge hardware, and tuning machines that have a recognizable look and feel. The pickup rings on a real Les Paul are made of quality plastic with consistent color that matches the era. The bridge and tailpiece have specific casting marks, plating quality, and weight that counterfeits don't bother to replicate accurately.

What fakes get wrong: Pickup rings that are too shiny, too matte, or the wrong shade of cream or black. Tuning machine buttons that feel cheap — the Kluson-style or Grover Rotomatic tuners on a real Gibson have a specific weight and smoothness. The bridge saddles on a fake often have rough casting marks or the wrong threading. The tailpiece studs may be the wrong diameter.

Hardware is hard to fake at scale because the real stuff is manufactured to tight tolerances. A fake can look right in photos but falls apart the moment you handle it.

07
Weight
Medium

Real Gibson Les Pauls are heavy. A vintage Standard typically weighs between 8 and 10 pounds, sometimes more. Modern weight-relieved models are lighter (7.5-9 lbs), but they still have a specific density and balance to them. The weight comes from the mahogany body with a carved maple cap — a combination that's expensive to replicate with the same species of wood.

What fakes get wrong: Many counterfeits use cheaper, lighter wood (often basswood or agathis) and come in at 6-7 pounds. Others overcompensate and use dense but wrong wood that makes the guitar heavier than it should be. Weight alone isn't proof of anything, but a Les Paul that weighs 6 pounds is either weight-relieved (which Gibson will document) or it's not real mahogany and maple.

What to do: Ask the seller for the exact weight. Any serious seller will have weighed it. If they can't tell you, that's a data point. If the weight doesn't match the model and era, start asking questions.

08
Neck Joint and Tenon
Hard

Gibson Les Pauls use a set neck (glued-in) construction with a mortise-and-tenon joint. On authentic guitars, the tenon extends deep into the body — a long tenon that contributes to the guitar's sustain and resonance. The neck joint is tight, with no visible gaps between the neck and body where they meet.

What fakes get wrong: A shorter, shallower tenon that doesn't extend as far into the body. Visible glue lines or gaps at the neck joint. The heel of the neck doesn't blend smoothly into the body — there's a step or ridge where it should be a seamless transition. Some counterfeits use bolt-on necks disguised with a glued cover plate, which is immediately obvious if you look inside the neck pickup cavity.

What to do: Run your fingers along the neck-to-body joint. It should be seamless. Look at the neck pickup cavity with a flashlight — you should be able to see the end of the tenon. If there are bolt holes or a clearly short tenon, walk away.

09
Electronics Cavity Routing
Hard

Open up the back control cavity cover. A real Gibson has clean, deliberate routing with specific shapes for the wiring channels between pickups, switch, and controls. The cavity is typically painted or shielded, and the wiring is neat with proper Gibson-spec components — CTS pots, Switchcraft jack, Switchcraft toggle switch.

What fakes get wrong: Sloppy routing with rough, irregular edges. Channels that are too wide or don't follow Gibson's standard pattern. Generic mini pots instead of full-size CTS pots. A cheap toggle switch with a thin, flimsy washer. Wiring that uses stranded wire where Gibson uses solid-core, or vice versa. The cavity might be too large, too small, or routed at the wrong depth.

What to do: Always ask for a photo of the electronics cavity. This is where counterfeits are hardest to fake, because getting the routing right requires the same CNC programs and the same components that Gibson uses. If the seller won't open the back, don't buy the guitar.

Gibson uses a specific control layout: two volume pots, two tone pots, a 3-way toggle, and an output jack. The wiring runs through routed channels in the body. On counterfeits, the channels are often drilled holes instead of proper routed channels.
10
Finish Quality and Edge Details
Hard

Gibson uses nitrocellulose lacquer on their higher-end models and polyurethane on some production models. Nitro finishes have a specific look — they're thinner, they check and crack with age in characteristic patterns, and they have a depth that poly finishes can't replicate. The sunburst on a real Les Paul has specific color transitions that Gibson has refined over decades.

What fakes get wrong: A thick, plasticky finish that sits on top of the wood rather than sinking into it. Sunburst colors that are too vivid or don't transition correctly — the cherry-to-amber fade on a '59 burst has a very specific gradient that most fakes get subtly wrong. Edge details where the finish meets the binding are often sloppy — the finish bleeds onto the binding, or the binding bleeds into the finish. On vintage models, the checking pattern (if present) looks artificial — too uniform, too deep, or in the wrong orientation.

What to do: Compare the finish in natural light against known-authentic examples. Look at the edges where the finish meets the binding and where it meets the fretboard. On the back, check where the finish meets the neck pocket — this is a detail that's hard to get right at a counterfeit price point.

Red Flags on Online Listings

You can learn a lot before you ever message the seller. These are the things that make experienced collectors close a tab immediately.

  • ! The price is too good. If a 1959 Les Paul Standard is listed at $40K when market is $250K+, it's either fake, stolen, or the seller is hiding something catastrophic. Even for modern Custom Shop models — a guitar listed 30-40% below market with no explanation is a trap.
  • ! Only 3-4 photos, all from the same angle. A real seller with a real $8,000 guitar takes 15+ photos because they want you to see everything. If you're looking at 3 carefully cropped shots, ask yourself what they're not showing you.
  • ! No headstock close-up. This is the single most revealing detail on a counterfeit. If the listing conveniently has no clear headstock shot, or it's always photographed at an angle that obscures the shape and logo, that's deliberate.
  • ! Stock photos mixed with real photos. Some scammers pull official Gibson marketing shots and mix them with 1-2 photos of the actual fake. If the lighting, background, or resolution changes between photos, you're looking at multiple sources.
  • ! The seller won't answer specific questions. Ask about weight, serial number, pickup resistance readings, fret life, any modifications. A real seller who owns a real guitar can answer these in their sleep. If you get vague answers or "it's in great condition," move on.
  • ! Shipping from regions known for counterfeits. This isn't about being unfair — it's about probability. If a "1959 Gibson Les Paul" is shipping from a region with known counterfeit guitar factories and the seller has no verifiable transaction history, the odds are not in your favor.
  • ! New seller account with premium listings. An account created two weeks ago listing a $15,000 guitar with no feedback history is a pattern that shows up in scam after scam. Real collectors accumulate reputation over time.
  • ! Insistence on off-platform payment. "I'll give you a better deal if you pay via Zelle/Venmo/wire transfer." This removes your buyer protection entirely. Every legitimate seller understands why buyers want platform protection on a high-value transaction.
Bottom Line

If your gut says something's off, it probably is. Every experienced collector has a story about the one they almost bought. The ones who got burned are the ones who talked themselves past the red flags because they wanted the guitar to be real.

What Even Good Fakes Get Right

Being honest about this matters. If you think all fakes are obvious, you're the exact person who gets fooled. Here's what the good counterfeits have figured out.

  • Overall body shape and proportions. The best fakes use CNC routing from scanned Gibson bodies. The outline is right. The cutaway depth is right. The body thickness is right. You can't tell from shape alone.
  • Color-matched finishes. High-end counterfeits now use color-matching software to replicate Gibson's specific sunburst gradients. In photos, they look identical. In person, under natural light, the depth of the color is usually where they fail — but it takes a trained eye.
  • Hardware that looks correct at a glance. The bridge, tailpiece, and tuners on a good fake look right in photos. They're the correct style, the correct color, and mounted in the correct positions. It's only when you handle them — feel the weight, test the smoothness, check the threading — that the difference becomes apparent.
  • Aging and relic work. The counterfeit market has gotten disturbingly good at artificial aging. Checking patterns, yellowed binding, wear marks in the right places. If someone hands you a "1959 Les Paul" that's been convincingly relic'd, finish checking alone won't save you. You need the full checklist.
  • Case and accessories. Some counterfeits now ship with convincing replica cases, COAs, and even hang tags. A case with a Gibson logo doesn't prove the guitar is real. A COA can be printed by anyone with a color laser printer.
Why This Matters

I'm not telling you this to scare you. I'm telling you this because the people who get burned are the people who think they only need to check one or two things. No single check is definitive. A real Gibson passes all 10 checks. A good fake might pass 6 or 7 of them. The only way to protect yourself is to run the full list.

When to Walk Away

Here's the hardest part: sometimes you find a guitar you really want, the price is right, and something doesn't add up. Maybe the serial number checks out but the binding nibs aren't there. Maybe the headstock looks right but the electronics cavity routing is sloppy. Maybe everything looks perfect in photos but the seller won't send you a video.

Walk away if:

  • Any two items on the checklist fail. One might be an anomaly or a repair. Two is a pattern.
  • The seller refuses to provide additional photos or video when asked. A person with a legitimate $8,000+ guitar has zero reason to hide details.
  • The price doesn't make sense and the seller can't explain why. "I need the money fast" is the oldest line in the book. Real sellers with real guitars know what they have.
  • Your gut tells you something is wrong and you can't articulate what it is. That feeling is your brain processing details your conscious mind hasn't caught up to yet. Trust it.
  • The seller pressures you to decide quickly. "I have two other buyers interested" may be true, but a real guitar will find a buyer regardless. Urgency is a manipulation tactic.

There is always another guitar. There is not always another $8,000 — or $15,000, or $20,000. The worst outcome isn't missing out on a deal. The worst outcome is owning a counterfeit that you paid real money for and can't resell without becoming the scammer yourself.

A Note on Getting Help

If you're not sure, ask someone who is. Bring a knowledgeable friend to see the guitar. Post photos in trusted collector forums (Les Paul Forum, MyLesPaul.com) and ask for opinions before you buy. Pay a qualified luthier $50-100 to inspect it. On a $10,000 purchase, that inspection is the best money you'll ever spend.
