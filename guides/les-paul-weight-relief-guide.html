Les Paul Weight Relief Explained: What It Means for Tone, Value & Playability

Wooden Valhalla February 2026 12 min read

If you've spent any time shopping for a Gibson Les Paul, you've run into the weight question. Maybe you've held a 1972 Custom that felt like a cinder block strapped to your shoulder. Maybe you picked up a 2015 Standard and thought something was wrong because it felt hollow. Or maybe you're staring at a Reverb listing that says "weight relieved" and you're wondering whether that's a feature or a red flag.

Here's the thing: weight matters to Les Paul players in a way it doesn't for most other guitars. A Strat weighs 7.5 pounds on a bad day. A Les Paul can hit 12. When you're standing for a four-hour gig or an eight-hour session, that difference isn't academic. It's the reason so many touring players swapped to SGs in the '60s, and it's the reason Gibson started routing material out of the body in the early '80s.

But weight isn't just about comfort. It's tied up in tone, sustain, resale value, and — if we're being honest — a fair amount of mythology. So let's cut through the marketing copy and the forum arguments and talk about what weight relief actually is, what it does, and what it means if you're buying, selling, or just trying to figure out what you already own.

The Body Types: Solid, Weight-Relieved, & Chambered

Gibson has used four distinct approaches to Les Paul body construction since the model's introduction in 1952. Each one represents a different answer to the same question: how do you balance weight, tone, and manufacturing cost? Understanding the differences is essential if you're evaluating a guitar's value or trying to predict how it'll sound and feel.

Type 01

Solid Body (Pre-1982 & Select Reissues)

The original and, for many collectors, the only "real" Les Paul body. A solid slab of mahogany with a carved maple cap on top, no routing beyond what's needed for pickups, controls, and the toggle switch. This is what a 1959 Standard is. It's what every Custom Shop Historic reissue is. And it's what most players are picturing when they imagine the Les Paul sound.

Solid-body Les Pauls typically weigh between 9 and 12 pounds, though there's wide variance. Mahogany is not a uniform wood — two pieces from the same tree can differ by a pound or more. The lightest vintage Les Pauls from the late '50s tend to run 8.5 to 9.5 pounds, partly because Gibson was using well-seasoned, lighter cuts of Honduran mahogany at the time. By the '70s, heavier pieces were common, and some Les Pauls from that era tip the scales past 11 or even 12 pounds.

  • Weight range: 9 - 12 lbs (vintage can be lighter, 8.5+)
  • Tone character: Maximum sustain, full low-end, rich harmonic complexity
  • Acoustic resonance: Strong — you can feel the guitar vibrate against your body
  • Era: 1952 - 1982, plus all Custom Shop Historics and true reissues
Type 02

Traditional Weight Relief (1982 - ~2007)

In the early 1980s, Gibson started drilling nine round holes into the mahogany body before gluing the maple cap on top. These holes — roughly 1 to 1.5 inches in diameter — are strategically placed in areas that don't interfere with the pickup cavities or control routes. From the outside, the guitar looks identical to a solid-body Les Paul. You can't see the holes. You can't access them. They're sealed under the maple top.

This approach typically removes about 1 to 2 pounds from the finished guitar, bringing most Les Pauls into the 8 to 9.5-pound range. It was Gibson's first real attempt to address the weight issue without fundamentally changing the instrument's character, and — I'll be direct about this — it's the approach that affects tone the least.

  • Weight range: 8 - 9.5 lbs
  • Tone character: Nearly indistinguishable from solid in a blind test
  • Acoustic resonance: Very slightly reduced — you'd struggle to notice unplugged
  • Era: ~1982 - 2007 (varies by model and year)

The honest take: I've A/B'd traditional weight-relieved Les Pauls against verified solid-body guitars through the same amp, same pickups, same cables. In a band mix? I could not consistently tell the difference. Unplugged, sitting on the couch? Maybe. Nine drilled holes in a big slab of mahogany just doesn't remove enough material to fundamentally change the acoustic properties of the instrument.

Type 03

Modern Weight Relief / Ultra Modern (2008+)

Around 2008, Gibson moved to a more aggressive weight relief pattern. Instead of nine simple round holes, they began routing a Swiss cheese-like pattern throughout the body — irregular shapes that remove significantly more wood. Some years use a pattern Gibson calls "Ultra Modern Weight Relief," which looks like a series of interconnected channels when you see an x-ray.

This approach gets Les Pauls down to 7 to 8.5 pounds, which is a meaningful reduction. A 7.5-pound Les Paul feels like a different instrument in your hands compared to a 10-pounder. It's comfortable on a strap in a way that heavier Les Pauls simply aren't.

But here's where opinions diverge. The more wood you remove, the more you change the instrument's acoustic properties. Modern weight-relieved Les Pauls have a different feel when you play them unplugged — the body vibrates differently, the attack can feel slightly less dense. Through an amp with gain, many players can't tell the difference. Clean, with your ear close to the body? It's there.

  • Weight range: 7 - 8.5 lbs
  • Tone character: Slightly more open midrange, subtly less low-end density
  • Acoustic resonance: Noticeably different unplugged — less mass vibrating
  • Era: 2008 - present (Standard, Classic, and other production models)
Type 04

Chambered (2008 - ~2012, Controversial)

This is the one that caused the most arguments. For several years, Gibson produced Les Paul models with large, open chambers routed into the mahogany body. Not small holes. Not Swiss cheese. Full chambers — some approaching the size of the cavities you'd find in a semi-hollow guitar. Gibson's Les Paul Standard was chambered from roughly 2008 to 2012, though exact years vary and Gibson's marketing wasn't always clear about which models used which construction.

Chambered Les Pauls can weigh as little as 7 pounds, sometimes less. They feel dramatically different from a solid Les Paul. Pick one up and your first reaction might be that something is missing. And acoustically, something is: mass.

The tonal difference with chambered bodies is real and noticeable. The acoustic response is airier, with less low-end thump and a more pronounced midrange character. Some players describe it as "woody" in a hollow-body way. Others say "thin." Neither is wrong — it depends on what you're comparing it to and what you want from the guitar.

  • Weight range: 6.5 - 8 lbs
  • Tone character: Noticeably airier, more midrange-forward, reduced low-end mass
  • Acoustic resonance: Approaches semi-hollow territory — you can feel the air inside
  • Era: ~2008 - 2012 (primarily Les Paul Standard; Gibson eventually moved away from this approach)

Why the controversy: Gibson didn't always make it obvious which guitars were chambered vs. weight-relieved. Buyers who expected a solid-body Les Paul and got one that weighed 7 pounds felt misled. The backlash was significant enough that Gibson eventually moved back toward less aggressive weight relief and started being more transparent about body construction in their marketing.

How Weight Relief Affects Tone

This is where you'll find the most heated forum debates, the most YouTube videos with clickbait titles, and the most confident opinions from people who've played exactly one Les Paul. So let me be straightforward about what I've experienced across dozens of Les Pauls with different body constructions.

Solid Body: The Reference Point

A solid-body Les Paul has the fullest low-end response, the most sustain, and the most complex harmonic overtones. When you hit a chord and let it ring, a solid Les Paul blooms in a way that lighter guitars don't. The notes stack and interact. The body itself contributes to the sound because there's enough mass to resonate sympathetically with the strings.

This is most obvious unplugged or through a clean amp. With distortion, the differences between body types compress dramatically — gain is a great equalizer.

Traditional Weight Relief: The Honest Non-Difference

I'm going to say something that might be unpopular: nine drilled holes in a mahogany slab do not meaningfully change the tone of a Les Paul. I've played traditional weight-relieved Les Pauls that sounded bigger, fatter, and more resonant than verified solid-body guitars. Wood selection, pickup winding, fret condition, nut material, and a dozen other factors have a larger impact on tone than nine 1.5-inch holes in the body.

If someone tells you they can hear the difference between a solid and a traditional weight-relieved Les Paul through a cranked Marshall, they're either lying or they've convinced themselves of something that isn't there. The math doesn't support it. You're removing maybe 5-8% of the body's mass. The maple cap, the pickups, the bridge, the tailpiece — all the primary tone-producing components are identical and unaffected.

Modern Weight Relief: The Detectable Shift

This is where the tonal conversation gets legitimate. Modern weight relief removes significantly more wood — 15-25% of the body mass in some cases. At that point, the physics start to matter. The body has less inertia, which means string vibration dissipates differently. In practice, this translates to:

  • A slightly more open, less compressed quality to the midrange
  • Marginally less low-end "weight" (pun intended) — the bass notes are there but they don't feel as thick
  • Sustain is slightly reduced on clean settings but functionally identical with gain
  • The attack can feel a touch snappier — less mass absorbing the initial transient

Is this better or worse? Neither. It's different. Some players prefer the slightly more articulate quality of a modern weight-relieved guitar. Some want the density of a solid body. Both are valid preferences, and both make great music.

Chambered: The Real Difference

Chambered Les Pauls sound noticeably different from solid ones, and I don't think that's controversial to anyone who's actually played both side by side. The large open chambers create an acoustic response that's closer to a semi-hollow guitar than a traditional Les Paul. You get:

  • A more prominent midrange "bark" with less low-end foundation
  • More acoustic volume unplugged — the chambers act like a small resonating cavity
  • A tendency toward feedback at high gain (not always in a good way)
  • A different "feel" when you're playing — the guitar vibrates against your body differently

If you're looking for the classic Les Paul tone — the thick, chewy, sustain-for-days sound — a chambered body isn't going to get you there. If you want something more open and lively, with a nod toward the semi-hollow world, some players love them. The problem wasn't the tone — it was the expectation mismatch.

How Weight Relief Affects Value

This is the section that matters most if you're buying or selling. The market has strong opinions about body construction, and those opinions directly affect what you'll pay and what you can sell for.

Solid Bodies Command a Premium

All else being equal, a solid-body Les Paul is worth more on the secondary market than a weight-relieved equivalent. This is true across every era. A 1978 Les Paul Custom with a verified solid body will sell for more than the same model with weight relief, even though both are great guitars. The premium ranges from a few hundred dollars on production models to thousands on vintage instruments.

Why? Partly tone, partly tradition, and partly perception. Collectors want what came first. They want the "real" thing. And for vintage guitars especially, solid body is the default expectation.

Collectors Prefer Unmodified Solid Bodies

In the vintage and collector market, body construction is near the top of the list behind originality and condition. A Les Paul that's had weight relief added after the fact — which some luthiers have done to reduce weight on heavy '70s guitars — takes a significant value hit. It's considered a modification, same as a refret or a refinish. If someone drilled holes in the body, it doesn't matter that the intent was "improvement." The market treats it as damage.

Custom Shop Reissues Are Solid for a Reason

Gibson's Custom Shop makes Historic reissues with solid bodies specifically because that's what the market demands. When someone pays $5,000 to $7,000 for an R9 or R0 reissue, they're paying for historical accuracy — and that means a solid mahogany body. The Custom Shop understands that weight relief would undermine the entire point of a reissue line.

The Exception: Player-Grade Market

If you're buying a Les Paul to play — not to collect, not to invest, just to make music — weight-relieved guitars are often a better value. They're priced lower specifically because of collector bias, which means you get a great-sounding instrument for less money. A 2005 Les Paul Standard with traditional weight relief is a phenomenal guitar that sells below where it should because of the collector premium on solid bodies.

How to Tell What You Have

Gibson hasn't always been transparent about which body construction a given guitar uses, and the information on spec sheets can be unreliable, especially for models produced between 2007 and 2014. Here are the methods that actually work.

1. Weight as Indicator

This is the fastest and most reliable screening method. Weigh the guitar on a postal scale or bathroom scale (hold the guitar, step on, subtract your weight). Then compare against the expected range:

Body Type Typical Weight If It Weighs... Confidence
Solid 9 - 12 lbs 10+ lbs? Almost certainly solid High
Traditional WR 8 - 9.5 lbs 8.5 - 9.5 lbs? Could be either solid (light wood) or traditional WR Medium
Modern WR 7 - 8.5 lbs Under 8 lbs? Very likely modern WR or chambered High
Chambered 6.5 - 8 lbs Under 7.5 lbs? Likely chambered Medium-High

Important caveat: Weight alone isn't definitive. I've held solid-body Les Pauls from the late '50s that weighed 8.4 pounds — the wood was just that light. And I've seen weight-relieved guitars from the '90s that still hit 9.5 because the mahogany was dense. Weight narrows the possibilities. It doesn't confirm them.

2. The Tap Test

Hold the guitar by the neck and tap the body in different spots — upper bout, lower bout, near the bridge, near the neck joint. A solid body produces a consistent, dense "thud" across the entire surface. Weight-relieved bodies may produce slightly different tones depending on whether you're tapping over a drilled hole or solid wood. Chambered bodies will sound noticeably more hollow and resonant when you tap over the chambers.

This isn't scientific. It's an art. But after you've tapped a few dozen Les Pauls, you develop an ear for it. The difference between solid and chambered is obvious to anyone. The difference between solid and traditional weight relief? Much harder to detect.

3. Serial Number and Year Ranges

Knowing the production year gives you a strong starting point. Here's the general breakdown:

Era Years Body Construction Notes
Golden Era 1952 - 1960 Solid All original Les Pauls are solid body
SG Transition 1961 - 1968 N/A Les Paul model discontinued (became SG)
Early Revival 1968 - 1981 Solid Heavier wood common; some 11-12 lb guitars
Weight Relief Era 1982 - ~2007 Traditional WR (9 holes) Some models remained solid; Custom Shop always solid
Chambered Period ~2008 - 2012 Chambered / Modern WR Standard was chambered; Classic used modern WR
Modern Era 2013 - present Modern WR / Ultra Modern WR More transparent about construction; Custom Shop still solid

4. X-Ray Imaging

Yes, some collectors actually x-ray their Les Pauls. It sounds extreme, but when you're spending $10,000+ on a guitar that's supposed to be a solid body, verifying construction is reasonable due diligence. A dental or veterinary x-ray machine will clearly show the internal routing pattern — solid, nine holes, Swiss cheese, or chambered.

You'll see x-ray images floating around guitar forums, especially for guitars from the transitional years (2007-2013) where Gibson's marketing and actual construction didn't always align. If you're buying a high-dollar Les Paul from this era and the seller claims it's a specific body type, asking for an x-ray isn't unreasonable. Any serious seller will understand the request.

5. Pickup Cavity Inspection

If you remove the pickups, you can sometimes see evidence of weight relief routing through the pickup cavities. This isn't always conclusive — the cavities are limited windows into the body — but on some models, the edge of a drilled hole or a routed channel is visible from the pickup route. This is particularly useful for guitars from the traditional weight relief era, where the nine holes are close enough to the pickup routes that one might be partially visible.

What I'd Actually Buy

After owning solid, weight-relieved, and chambered Les Pauls, here's my honest take on what I'd spend my money on. Your priorities might be different — that's fine. But this is what I've landed on after years of buying, selling, playing, and comparing.

If You're a Collector

Buy solid body. Every time. If the guitar is going to sit in a case as much as it's played, if you care about resale value, if you want the historically correct construction — solid is the only answer. Custom Shop Historics are the sweet spot for most collectors: solid body, correct specs, consistent quality, and a price point that's high but not vintage-market insane. A Murphy Lab R9 with a solid body is a better financial decision than a weight-relieved Standard that you paid less for but will depreciate more.

If You're a Working Player

Traditional weight-relieved Les Pauls from the 1990s and 2000s are the best deal in the Les Paul market right now. You get 95% of the tone at 85% of the weight, and you pay significantly less because collectors have driven the prices down. A 2003 Les Paul Standard with traditional weight relief is a phenomenal instrument. It'll weigh 8.5 to 9 pounds, it'll sound like a Les Paul, and you can gig with it four nights a week without needing a chiropractor.

If You Have Back or Shoulder Issues

Modern weight-relieved Les Pauls are genuinely comfortable in a way that solid bodies aren't. A 7.5-pound Les Paul is a different experience on a strap. If you're dealing with physical limitations and you want the Les Paul look and most of the tone, don't let forum purists talk you out of a lighter guitar. The tonal difference through a cranked amp is minimal. Your body will thank you.

What I'd Avoid

Chambered Les Pauls from the 2008-2012 era are the hardest sell in the current market. They're not bad guitars — some sound genuinely great in the right context. But they carry the stigma of the chambering controversy, they don't hold value well, and the tonal difference from a solid body is significant enough that they don't scratch the same itch. If you find one cheap enough and you like how it sounds, go for it. But don't pay Standard prices expecting a Standard tone.

The bottom line: The "best" Les Paul body type is the one that matches your priorities. If you care most about tone and value, go solid. If you care most about playability and comfort, go weight-relieved or modern. If someone tries to tell you there's only one right answer, they're selling you something — probably a solid-body Les Paul.
